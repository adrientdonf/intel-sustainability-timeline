The RISE Strategy

Responsible. Inclusive. Sustainable. Enabling.

Launched in 2021, Intel's RISE strategy defines a framework for responsible corporate behavior across four key pillars. It is not aspirational — it is measurable, with 2030 targets attached to each commitment and third-party audits verifying progress every year.

Every chip Intel designs, every facility it builds, every partner it works with is evaluated through this lens. The goal is a world where the technology that powers human progress does not come at the expense of the planet that sustains it.

Five Decades of Progress

The Sustainability
Timeline

From founding principles to cutting-edge commitments — explore how Intel's environmental journey has evolved since 1968.

1968
Intel Founded

The Founding Ethos

Robert Noyce and Gordon Moore established Intel in Mountain View, California. From the very first day, the company's culture was defined by engineering rigor and a sense of responsibility — both to shareholders and to the communities where factories would be built.

Origin
1994
First Report

First Environmental Report

Intel published its first corporate Environmental Health and Safety report — one of the earliest in the semiconductor industry. This marked a deliberate shift from informal stewardship to structured, auditable environmental accountability that third parties could scrutinize and verify.

Reporting
2003
Green Power

Renewable Energy Commitment

Intel became the first semiconductor company in the Fortune 500 to make a large-scale renewable energy purchase commitment. The company bought millions of kilowatt-hours of wind energy, demonstrating that manufacturing scale and clean energy could coexist economically.

Energy
2012
LEED Certified

LEED-Certified Campuses

Intel's facilities in Chandler, Arizona, and Hillsboro, Oregon, achieved LEED Platinum certification — the highest standard in sustainable building design. Smart HVAC systems, recycled water cooling, and solar-ready rooftops reduced facility energy consumption by over 30% compared to conventional construction.

Infrastructure
2020
RISE Strategy

RISE Strategy Launched

Intel unveiled the most comprehensive corporate responsibility framework in its history — the RISE strategy. With explicit 2030 targets across responsible sourcing, inclusive hiring, sustainable manufacturing, and enabling technology, RISE transformed Intel's sustainability work from program-level initiatives to company-wide imperatives with board-level accountability.

Milestone

Net Zero Water

Net Positive Water & 90% Clean Energy

By end of 2023, Intel achieved net positive water status in all water-stressed regions where it operates — restoring more water than it uses. Simultaneously, 90% of global electricity consumption came from renewable sources, putting Intel on track for its 100% target by 2030.

The Horizon

The 2030 Horizon

By 2030, Intel has committed to 100% renewable electricity, net positive water across all operations, zero waste to landfill, and reaching 4 billion people with responsible technology. These aren't aspirations — they are contractually embedded into executive compensation and reported annually with third-party verification.

Technology for Good

Innovations That
Changed the Equation

Intel doesn't just make faster chips — it engineers efficiency, longevity, and responsibility into every generation of silicon.

01

Moore's Law & Energy Efficiency

Gordon Moore's 1965 prediction — that transistor density would double every two years — has driven 55 years of energy efficiency gains that no renewable energy project could match. Each generation of Intel chips delivers the same computing performance with a fraction of the power consumption of the previous one.

Today's Intel Core Ultra processors use up to 50% less energy per unit of computation than chips made just a decade ago. Across the billions of devices running Intel silicon, that represents a reduction in electricity demand equivalent to taking millions of cars off the road.

50% Less energy per computation vs. 2013 chips
02

Extreme Ultraviolet Lithography

EUV lithography — the process Intel uses to etch billions of transistors onto chips using light with wavelengths shorter than an atom's diameter — is one of the most precise manufacturing techniques in human history. Beyond enabling smaller transistors, it dramatically reduces the number of manufacturing steps required to build a chip.

Fewer steps mean less chemical waste, less water use, less electricity consumed per wafer. Intel's adoption of EUV at high volume was driven as much by sustainability math as by performance gains — a rare alignment of financial and environmental incentives.

13.5nm Wavelength of EUV light — shorter than a DNA strand
03

3D Chip Packaging & Foveros

Intel's Foveros 3D packaging technology stacks chiplets vertically — like floors in a skyscraper — rather than spreading transistors flat across a die. This allows engineers to mix and match specialized chiplets optimized for performance or efficiency, reducing waste by using only what's needed for each specific task.

The result is a new class of processors that adapt their power draw in real time. When running light workloads, the efficiency chiplets take over and reduce consumption dramatically. This kind of adaptive efficiency is key to Intel's goal of reducing the energy footprint of AI computing, which currently accounts for a growing share of global electricity use.

Foveros 3D stacking for adaptive, task-aware power efficiency
04

Responsible Mineral Sourcing

Modern processors require tantalum, tungsten, tin, and gold — minerals often extracted from conflict regions. Intel pioneered industry-wide conflict mineral disclosure standards, and has been the only semiconductor company to receive a "conflict-free" validation from the Conflict-Free Sourcing Initiative every year since 2013.

This commitment extends to 100% of Intel's supply chain, including thousands of sub-tier suppliers across 50+ countries. Intel's Supplier Responsibility program trains, audits, and holds accountable every partner that touches its product — before a single chip is built.

2030 & Beyond

The Future
Intel Is Building

Intel's 2030 commitments are more than targets — they are a redesign of what manufacturing, computing, and corporate responsibility look like in the 21st century.

🌍

Climate Action

Net zero greenhouse gas emissions across Scope 1, 2, and 3 value chain. Intel is investing in direct air capture, carbon offsetting with permanence standards, and process chemistry that eliminates fluorinated gases — the most potent GHGs in chip manufacturing.

2040
⚡

100% Clean Energy

Every kilowatt-hour of electricity powering Intel's global manufacturing — from Arizona to Ireland to Malaysia — will come from renewable sources. Intel is building direct power purchase agreements with solar and wind farms near each major facility.

2030
💧

Water Positive

Intel will restore more water than it consumes across all operations — including water-stressed regions in the American Southwest and Southeast Asia. Proprietary water recycling at Intel's fabs now achieves recirculation rates above 80%.

2030
♾️

Circular Economy

Zero manufacturing waste to landfill, and a supply chain designed for reuse, refurbishment, and material recovery. Intel is developing chemical processes to extract precious metals from end-of-life chips — reducing mining demand by closing the material loop.
